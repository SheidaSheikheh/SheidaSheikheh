A finished storage cavern looks simple from the surface — just a wellhead in a
field. Underground, it's a carefully engineered system, and every part has a job.

## The parts

From the valves at the surface to the dead space at the very bottom, here's what
makes up a working hydrogen cavern.

The single most important detail is the **last cemented casing shoe**: the depth
where the steel-and-cement well ends and the open cavern begins. Its pressure
integrity sets the safe limits for the entire store.

## Cushion gas vs. working gas

Not all the hydrogen in a cavern can be sold. A permanent **cushion** holds the
pressure up — keeping the walls supported and the deliverability high — while only
the **working gas** above it is actually cycled.

<Callout type="key" title="Why the cushion is non-negotiable">
Run a cavern down to empty and two things go wrong: the rock starts to creep
inward, and the remaining gas can't be delivered fast enough. The cushion is the
floor that prevents both. It can be a large share of the total inventory — a real
cost of doing business underground.
</Callout>

## How it's operated

Day to day, operating a cavern means walking its pressure up and down inside a safe
window — high enough to resist creep, low enough to avoid fracturing the rock or
breaching the caprock.

<Figure caption="The safe operating window as a function of depth. Deeper caverns get a higher, wider window — and therefore hold more hydrogen.">
  <OperatingPressureWindow client:visible />
</Figure>

Each injection and withdrawal is a trip across that window. How fast and how often
you can make that trip — the cavern's *deliverability* and *cycling rate* — is what
makes salt caverns so well suited to balancing a renewable grid.

<Callout type="key" title="Takeaways">
- A cavern is a full system: **wellhead, cemented casing, casing shoe, cavern body, and sump.**
- The **casing shoe** governs pressure integrity for everything below it.
- **Cushion gas** stays put; only **working gas** is cycled.
- Operation means staying inside a depth-dependent **pressure window**.
</Callout>
