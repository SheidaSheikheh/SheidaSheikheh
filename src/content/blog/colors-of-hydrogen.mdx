Hydrogen is the same colorless gas no matter how you make it. But you'll constantly
hear it described in color — green, blue, grey, pink, turquoise. Those names are a
shorthand for the *production route*, and that's what determines whether a
kilogram of hydrogen is a climate solution or a climate problem.

## A spectrum of production

<Figure wide caption="Pick a color to see its feedstock, process, and rough CO2 intensity.">
  <HydrogenColors client:visible />
</Figure>

The headline: most hydrogen made today is **grey** — steam-reforming natural gas,
emitting roughly ten kilograms of CO₂ for every kilogram of hydrogen. **Green**
hydrogen (electrolysis run on renewables) is the goal, with **blue** (reforming
plus carbon capture) and **turquoise** (methane pyrolysis to solid carbon) as
lower-carbon bridges.

<Callout type="key" title="Why the color matters">
Clean hydrogen only helps if it's actually clean. Switching from grey to green is
the entire point — but green hydrogen needs cheap renewable power *and* somewhere
to store the output, because supply and demand rarely line up in time.
</Callout>

## The catch: storing it

Whatever its color, hydrogen shares an awkward physical trait. It packs more energy
per kilogram than any other fuel — and almost the least per litre.

<Figure caption="Switch between energy per mass and per volume. Hydrogen tops the first chart and sits near the bottom of the second.">
  <EnergyDensityChart client:visible />
</Figure>

That mismatch is the whole reason underground storage exists. You can't keep
meaningful amounts of hydrogen in tanks; you need the volume of a cavern or a
reservoir. So the color question — *how do we make it cleanly?* — leads straight to
a storage question — *where do we keep it?* — which is exactly where the geology of
salt and trona comes in.

<Callout type="key" title="Takeaways">
- The "colors" describe **how hydrogen is made**, not the gas itself.
- **Grey** dominates today; **green** is the goal; **blue/turquoise** are bridges.
- Hydrogen is energy-dense **per kilogram** but sparse **per litre**.
- That low volumetric density is why clean hydrogen needs **large-scale underground storage.**
</Callout>
