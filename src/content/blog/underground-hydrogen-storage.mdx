If hydrogen is going to do real work in a clean-energy grid, we need to store a
lot of it for a long time. Not a few hours of buffering — *months*. A wind-and-solar
grid makes too much energy in some seasons and not enough in others, and the only
place big and cheap enough to soak up that mismatch is the ground beneath our feet.

This post is about the practical questions that follow: **what kind of rock can hold
hydrogen, how much fits, what pressures are involved, and how an engineer decides
where to put it.**

## Where can hydrogen go underground?

There are four serious candidates, and they trade off against each other. Salt
caverns cycle fast and stay tight but each one is modest in size. Depleted gas
reservoirs are enormous but slow and cushion-gas hungry. Aquifers are a wildcard.
Lined rock caverns are flexible but pricey.

</Figure>

There's no universal winner — only the right tool for a given geology and duty
cycle. Salt is the safe bet for fast, clean cycling; depleted reservoirs win when
you simply need somewhere vast.

## The footprint argument

Why bother going underground at all? Because the numbers above ground don't work.
The energy you can store per unit of surface area is wildly different.

<Figure caption="Energy stored per site, on a logarithmic scale. Note that each step to the right is ten times larger.">
  <CapacityVsFootprint client:visible />
</Figure>

A single salt cavern can hold something like a thousand times the hydrogen of a
surface tank — from roughly the same patch of ground. That ratio is the entire
reason underground storage exists.

## Storing the seasons

Seasonal storage is the use case that makes geologic storage essential. Charge the
store when renewable power is plentiful; draw it down when demand spikes and the
wind is calm.

<Figure wide caption="A year of seasonal storage. Press play and watch the inventory fill through summer and empty through winter — while the cushion gas never moves.">
  <SeasonalStorageChart client:visible />
</Figure>

<Callout type="key" title="Working gas vs. cushion gas">
Not all the hydrogen in a store can be used. A permanent **cushion** keeps the
pressure high enough to hold the cavern open and maintain deliverability. Only the
**working gas** above that cushion is cycled. Designing a store means sizing both.
</Callout>

## How deep, how much pressure?

The amount of hydrogen a cavern holds is set by pressure, and pressure is set by
depth. Too low and the rock creeps inward; too high and you risk fracturing it.
The safe band sits between roughly 30% and 80% of the lithostatic load.

<Figure caption="The operating-pressure window as a function of depth. Drag the depth slider — deeper caverns get a higher, wider safe window, and so hold more gas.">
  <OperatingPressureWindow client:visible />
</Figure>

This is why depth is such a prized property in site screening: it directly buys you
capacity. It's also why Wyoming's shallow trona beds are a tougher engineering
problem than deep salt.

## Choosing a site

Real site selection is never one number. It's a balance of geology, safety, and
geography — depth, seal integrity, reservoir quality, seismic calm, and how far the
storage sits from where hydrogen is made and used. Different priorities crown
different winners.

<Figure caption="A weighted site-selection model for three Wyoming basins. Slide the weights to match your priorities and watch the ranking change.">
  <SiteSelectionCriteria client:visible />
</Figure>

This is exactly the kind of screening that underpins underground-storage planning
in Wyoming: lay out the candidate basins, score them honestly, and be explicit
about what you're optimising for.

<Callout type="key" title="Takeaways">
- Hydrogen for a clean grid must be stored **underground, seasonally, at scale**.
- The four main options — salt caverns, depleted reservoirs, aquifers, lined rock
  caverns — each win under different conditions.
- **Depth buys capacity** by widening the safe pressure window.
- Site selection is a transparent, weighted trade-off — not a single best answer.
</Callout>
